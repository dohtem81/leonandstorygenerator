Leon stared at the screen for a long time.

— "Finish it yourself"… great — he muttered. — So no dragons, no doors, no hints?

The Storygenerator blinked just once. Quietly. As if truly handing over the reins.

Leon leaned back in his chair.

And then he understood something strange.

This wasn't a story he was meant to enter.

This was a story he was already… living.

He looked around his room. At the books, the unfinished drawing, the piece of cardboard left over from building the ship.

— So what… I'm supposed to make something up?

Silence.

— Okay… let's try a different approach.

He got up and went to the desk. He picked up a sheet of paper.

At the top he wrote:

"The Story of a Boy and the Storygenerator"

He stopped.

— A bit meta… but alright.

He began to write.

About the first click. About the meadow and the flying fish. About the pancake castle. About the shadow that stole endings. About the boy who didn't believe in his drawing. About the ship that fell apart… and was built again.

He wrote slowly.

Sometimes he stopped.

Sometimes he crossed something out.

But he didn't stop.

Until at last he reached the final line.

He hesitated.

— Alright… the ending.

He looked at the Storygenerator.

— Now what?

The screen was blank.

But this time… that didn't bother him.

Leon smiled slightly and wrote:

"And then the boy understood that the Storygenerator had never really been creating stories. It had only been reminding him that they were already inside him."

At that moment the Storygenerator shone more brightly than all the other times.

— Ending accepted.

— But — another line appeared — this is not the end.

Leon raised an eyebrow.
— Seriously?

Something new appeared on the screen.

Not a level.

Not a task.

Just one sentence:

— Would you like to create a story for someone else?

Leon thought of the boy with the dragon. Of his brother. Of all the stories that might get stuck if no one helped them move forward.

He smiled.

— Yes.

The Storygenerator blinked softly.

And this time… it didn't take him anywhere.

Because the doors had changed.

They were no longer inside the device.

They were everywhere.

In every question.

In every moment.
