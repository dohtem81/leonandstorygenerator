Leon came back to the ship together with the dragon, who was still trying to smuggle in… something that looked like a "portable campfire in a jar."

— Leave it — said Leon, closing the hatch. — Seriously.

— But it's a travel edition! — the dragon protested.

The Storygenerator glowed more calmly than usual.

— New adventure: The Story of a Boy and Anger.

Leon sat in the seat.
— Okay… that sounds less funny.

— But very important.

Click.

This time there was no space.

No dragons (well, apart from the one beside him trying not to breathe fire).

There was an ordinary house.

An ordinary room.

And a boy.

He stood at his desk, fists clenched.

— They always do that! — he cried. — They never listen to me!

Leon glanced at the Storygenerator.
— Well… I know this feeling.

— Warning: strong emotions. Do not try to "switch them off."

Leon nodded.
— Of course.

He stepped closer.
— Hey…

The boy spun around sharply.
— What?!

— Easy — said Leon. — I can see you're angry.

— YES I AM! — he shouted. — Because they told me to turn off my game and I was in the middle of it!

Leon nodded.
— Classic.

— And they say I can "do it later" — the boy scoffed. — They don't understand!

Leon sat down beside him.
— True. Sometimes they don't understand.

The boy looked at him in surprise.
— Really?

— Really.

A moment of silence.

— So what am I supposed to do? — the boy asked, a little more quietly now. — Because I feel like… I don't know… slamming the door or something.

The dragon whispered to Leon:
— I usually breathe fire.

Leon looked at him.
— Not helping.

Leon looked at the boy.
— You know what… anger itself isn't the problem.

— How is it not?

— It's like a warning light. It says: "hey, something matters to me."

The boy frowned.
— Meaning?

— Meaning that the fact you got upset means you cared about that game.

— Yeah, I did!

— But what you do with that anger… that's your choice.

The boy looked at his hands.
— So I shouldn't shout?

Leon shrugged.
— Shouting happens. To everyone. The question is: does it help?

The boy thought.
— …not really.

— Exactly.

The dragon raised his paw.

— Maybe pancakes?

— That's not the solution to everything — said Leon.

— It's underrated.

Leon stood up and picked up a sheet of paper.

— Right. Let's do something.

He drew a simple diagram.

— Here's you.

— Here are your parents.

— And here… is what you want to say.

The boy looked.
— So?

— Try saying it without shouting. In a way they can actually hear.

— But I'm angry!

— I know. So say it with the anger, but without attacking.

The boy took a deep breath.

— Okay…

He went to the door.

He stopped.

— What if they don't listen?

Leon smiled gently.
— Then at least you'll know you handled it better than before.

The boy nodded.

He opened the door.

— Mum… Dad… — he began. — I know you told me to turn it off, but I was in the middle of a match and it mattered to me. Can I next time finish the round?

Silence.

Leon and the dragon listened carefully.

— …okay, I understand — came a voice from behind the door. — Next time let us know sooner.

The boy came back.

— It worked… — he said, surprised.

Leon smiled.
— See.

— But I'm still a little angry.

— And that's okay too.

The Storygenerator glowed softly:
— Mission complete: anger used, not exploded.

The dragon clapped his paws.
— And now pancakes to calm down?

The boy laughed for the first time.
— Maybe.

Leon stood up.
— See? It's not about not feeling angry.

— Only?

— About not letting it control you too much.

The boy nodded.
— That's hard.

— The most important things usually are.

Walking back to the ship, Leon looked at the Storygenerator.

— You know what… that was harder than the dragon and space.

— Because it's real.

Leon sat in the seat.

— Well… a little very much so.

The dragon had already lit his mini-campfire (illegally).

— So what's next?

Leon smiled gently.
— I have a feeling that every next story will be a little more… about people.
